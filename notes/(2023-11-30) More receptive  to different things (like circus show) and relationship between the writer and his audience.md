It's another uneventful day again, I'm going to have 2 consecutive classes so I expected myself to be exhausted at the end of the day. I'm thinking of doing one novel thing a day and I don't know where to find it. What is a novel thing to me? I came across a promo for a circus show in the cultural center nearby, is it a novel thing? If I watch a brand new TV show, is it novel? I supposed novelty is defined by two things:

- (1) First is the kind of things that I've never thought of doing, and 
- (2) Second is the kind of things that I'm against in the past, but somewhat open to it now.

I've never been to a circus show before, so that is novel. I do watch a lot of TV show but I rarely want to watch a K-drama. If I do, I'd count it as a novel thing. Nevertheless, there are lots of novel things. I have an idea that I'd take a tour around HCMC but only with buses, I'd stop at location of interest, wander around a little bit and take the next bus. That's neat, but I need a full day to do it. Upcoming January and February, my schedule will be vacant due to empty classes so maybe, I'll try it then.

Also, today I've read something interesting, actually I've several captivating articles and blog post today but I'd like to limit my writing to once a day. This post is about writing, and how writing isn't actually about you but about your audience. This is a concept that I've explored before, the incentive to write for oneself is different from the incentive to write for an audience, for once, you want to please an audience, and therefore continually do some micro adjustments to fit your writing (be it blog post, poem, YT video script, movie script, etc) to the taste of that audience. Thus, your posts gradually shift from being a reflection of yourself to the reflection of the audience itself. As the author put it:

>I struggled to keep the expectations from steering my writing even when I wasn’t writing for an audience. Their expectations had become mine. And this made it hard for me to follow my thoughts where they wanted to go.

This is my position too, until I read the rest of his post. Yes, the expectation of the audience will shape you, but you can also choose what audience to entertain.

>But I no longer think that it is quite right. The relationship between communal writing and self-writing is more complex than that. There is often conflict between the needs of the writer and the needs of the audience. But there can also be mutuality. An audience that is right at one time might be wrong at another. The literary world unleashed me when I was twenty and began distorting me at 23.

His perception of writing in the context of having an audience is a communal process, or aptly named **communal writing**. There will be conflict but there will also be mutuality. In his essay, there was no mention of how to find such audience but I guess that is so individualized that each person have to find their own.
