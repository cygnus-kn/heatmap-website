I recently went back to my hometown to help my family organize a get-together, it’s a popular tradition in Vietnam. On a day like this we cook a lot of food as an offering to the has-been, it also serves as a feast for everyone who turns up to do their offering. My family hosts around three get-togethers a year so we own bunch of dishes, cutleries, chairs and tables. After some cooking I went to set up the food on the altar. Usually, I would use a chair to reach the upper altar so I grasped one from a newly purchased stash. Upon standing on it I felt slightly nervous because the chair flexed too much, it felt like the chair is paper thin, this is weird because newer chairs should be sturdier than the old ones, is it not? Or was I gaining weight, …which is something plausible, …not gonna lie. Anyway, I looked around for old chairs and took one out of a stash. It surprisingly well-build, better than the newer ones, putting them side by side and pushing my hand hard against the surface of both chairs, it does confirm that the new chair is way thinner and as a result, flex more than the old one. This caught my attention less because I did get into similar situations with other common household items in my house. The old dishes is also thicker and heavy than the new one. The old spoons and chopsticks are both of more polished in quality and thicker than newer ones. If then can make it with less materials and more durability I would understand but for common items like a spoon, a chair or a pair of chopsticks, sturdier build or thicker width always translates to quality and durability. It seems that newer items manufactured today uses less raw materials. But question of “Is it actually lessen the quality of the product?” itself is quite hard to objectively conclude. Anecdotally, I do believe that the quality does goes down. The more I think about, and come across evident that somewhat confirm my suspicion, the firmer I believe that there is an actual argument for degradation of new things. Here is an attempt to elaborate my position, there are three trends that I think, contribute to the phenomenon.

- Cost cutting as a sign of mature market  
- Adoption of planned obsolescence.
- Survivor bias.

As always, shift in the quality of product, which involves a capital-intensive industry behind the scene, would move slowly, and affected by many aspects in each step of the production and consumption over a long period. These changes can never be attributed to a singularity event but rather a gradual process that would develop further into the furture as well. So the kind of condition that leads to the degradation of new things 
That’s why you don’t seems to see the three reasons I listed fit on one paradigm.
