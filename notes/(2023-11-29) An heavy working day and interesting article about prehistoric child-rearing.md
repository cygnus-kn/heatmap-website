Also, I've read several interesting articles today, one of which talked about how evolutionarily, children in their youth are psychologically developed to interact with many people in the role of caregivers.

>In the observed hunter-gatherer communities, caregiving goes so far beyond the parents, according to the researchers. Children often have 10+ caregivers and occasionally 20 or more, and typically a mother's support system would help respond to more than half of her baby's episodes of crying, which can be one of the most challenging aspects of parenting.

This is beneficial to the children and the actual parents too, since the children possessed a reduced risk of neglect and abuse and as for parent, they get to share the stress of raising children and improve their well-being, thus improving the relationship with the children also. This is in contrast with the condition of modern child rearing whereby:

>The authors also point out that in Western societies it is common for childcare to be used simply to allow parents to work, but insist that childcare needs to give parents an actual break. They argue that throughout human history and prehistory, parents have never been under the pressure they are now in terms of lack of support.

This reminded me of my childhood, especially in the context of Vietnam, we do have the concept of extended family and often times, me and children of my parents' siblings are grouped together to be tended by the whole extended family, and therefore better developed in our social roles.
